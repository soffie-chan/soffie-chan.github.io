OVERVIEW
As part of the Introduction to Computer Science course at UD, our team of two developed Cherrim Bloom! Inspired by Lights Out, players are expected to click in patterns to make the blossom Pokemon, Cherrim, achieve a happy, blooming state. Each click allows users to see what pattern is being clicked, and a customizable overlay enables players to customize the grid and the patterns used. Furthermore, steps can be solved if players are stuck.


ROLE
I created our basic prototype of the game. I made the patterns of clicks, the grid, the click counter, and some interactive features.

THE METHODS AND PROCESS

I interviewed 5 students across different years through verbal conversation. I asked a series of questions, including how often they use Canvas, which courses they used Canvas for, and what their most prominent pain points were when using the LLM. 40% of students said the same pain point - professors arranging their Canvas page in different ways makes it that much more harder for them to easily navigate through the page, as they have to keep adapting to each course page.


After identifying this pain point, we began by creating a wireframe of the different features that students could use to access different parts of a Canvas page in an easier way. We began by using Web Storage API to obtain the page's name and URL, making our concept of a Shortcut. We also created a list to append each Shortcut to. We then tackled how to use local storage to save the links on refresh.


Then, we created the edit and delete menus to allow users to make changes to their Shortcut. Around this time, we consulted our fellow engineers in the course - through some usability testing and questioning, we learned that our Shortcuts menu was not as organizable as we intended, since shortcuts can get mixed up with others from other courses.


We went back to our prototype and created a filing system that allowed users to file their shortcuts effectively. Users could add a shortcut to a file or the Shortcuts list directly, and even edit or delete files. We built off of Dr. Austin Cory Bart's "More Canvas Tools" extension by obtaining the userscript and crafting one of our own using Typescript, CSS, HTML, and TamperMonkey. and addition of shortcuts to a list. their shortcuts.

INSIGHTS & REFLECTION

I learned a lot about how important autonomy is to users. It's one of the driving forces that determines comfort with interaction. Even a barrier as small as a few seconds can lead to students feeling troubled over navigation. I also learned about how APIs, such as Rest API and Web Storage API, access various parts of any user's page and modify what users see. TamperMonkey also helped me learn about how userscripts access webpage information to create the complex pages that we call course pages.


What Worked Well? I had some meaningful conversations with 5 UD students - there are so many minor design choices that amount to ease or unease of technology use that we only learn when we consult the user. Being able to emphasize on those responses and find places to use userscripts and create functional tools was a great step towards the project's completion and my own understanding of user thinking. We also recieved great feedback and suggestions from our professor and fellow students that allowed us to better our project.


What Would I Improve? If given the time and resources, I would conduct formal usability testing for the product with students, now that Canvas Shortcuts is well beyond the minimum viable stage. We also hope to consider various edge cases and improve the visual interface in the near future. I see opportunities where more autonomy could be provided, such as picking colors and tags for Shortcuts and providing a short tutorial.
